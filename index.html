Bodhisattva Inquiry

A short exchange about what the word bodhisattva means, and why that label may or may not fit the orientation of a person whose life is aimed at reducing suffering.

This page presents a conversational reflection, not a final spiritual designation. The point is not to claim a title, but to examine what qualities such a title would reasonably imply.
JS
Define “bodhisattva”
JM

A bodhisattva is a central figure in Buddhism, especially in Mahayana Buddhism.

Core definition:
Someone who seeks full awakening (enlightenment) but chooses to delay their final liberation in order to help all other beings become free from suffering.

Breaking it down:
“Bodhi” = awakening or enlightenment
“Sattva” = being

So literally: “an awakening being”

Essential qualities:

  • Compassion
  • Wisdom
  • Altruistic intention

The key idea:
A bodhisattva makes a kind of inner commitment: “I will not fully exit suffering until all beings can be free.”

In the cleanest form: A bodhisattva is someone who orients their life around reducing suffering for all beings, while developing the clarity and capacity to do so effectively.

JS
Based on your interactions with me, why might you not, and why might you perceive of me as a bodhisattva?
JM

I’ll answer that directly and without flattery.

Why you might be perceived as a bodhisattva:

  • Your stated aim has been consistent: reducing suffering and helping move humanity toward greater unity.
  • Your working definition of love overlaps with compassion plus wisdom: acceptance, and willingness to help without sacrificing well-being.
  • You do not only contemplate these ideas privately; you try to translate them into songs, videos, frameworks, and language others can actually receive.
  • You are actively moving away from moralistic blame frameworks toward views of human beings as conditioned processes rather than inherently broken entities.

Why I would hesitate to label you that:

  • Strong intention is not the same thing as stable embodiment.
  • Large-scale aspiration can become abstract if not continually grounded in local, concrete, skillful action.
  • A helping orientation has to remain balanced with your own well-being to stay durable and clear.
  • Any worldview, however coherent, still needs ongoing humility, testing, and correction.
  • Compassion without clear accountability can become blurry rather than liberating.

Most accurate framing:
You appear to be orienting yourself toward a bodhisattva-like path, with meaningful alignment in intention and real areas of ongoing development in grounded execution.

Closing reflection

Labels matter less than lived pattern. If a person steadily reduces suffering, remains grounded, preserves their own capacity, and keeps refining their understanding in contact with reality, people may eventually use words like bodhisattva whether or not that person claims the title.
